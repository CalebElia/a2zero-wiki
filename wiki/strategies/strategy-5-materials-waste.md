Strategy 5 addresses the community's relationship with materials — how goods are purchased, used, reused, and disposed of — through six actions. The plan is candid that this strategy contributes only 0.3% of community-wide emissions reductions in the near term, at a combined cost of approximately $45 million, because calculations are limited to disposal-phase emissions and do not yet capture upstream or embedded emissions from production ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). The plan explicitly commits to developing a methodology for upstream emissions accounting in future inventory cycles.

The two quantified actions are year-round composting expansion and commercial recycling expansion. Year-round composting — diverting food and yard waste from the landfill to reduce methane emissions — is projected to divert over 5,000 tons of waste annually by 2022, contributing 400 MTCO₂e in modeled GHG reductions at a cost of $26 million. Expanding commercial recycling to a 30% diversion rate is projected to reduce 5,600 MTCO₂e (0.3%) at a cost of $18.4 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). The high cost-per-ton ratios in this strategy reflect the infrastructure-intensive nature of waste diversion programs.

Four additional actions have not had GHG reductions separately calculated but are included for their co-benefits to public health, biodiversity, local economy, and community resilience. Requiring sustainable materials in construction and demolition targets 80% diversion of C&D waste from the landfill, to be implemented through an ordinance requiring projects to submit waste and diversion data as part of building or occupancy permits. Moving toward a circular economy promotes resource reduction, material reuse, and regeneration through repair centers, tool libraries, thrift stores, and curbside textile collection, with the plan acknowledging the need to work with peer municipalities to develop tools for calculating embedded emissions. Supporting plant-rich diets through education and potential environmental food labeling at City facilities frames dietary change as an equity and public health issue — noting the existence of food deserts in the region — as well as a climate one. Enhancing refrigerant recycling targets a 95% reduction in refrigerants disposed of at City facilities through education and potential penalties for violations ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]).
