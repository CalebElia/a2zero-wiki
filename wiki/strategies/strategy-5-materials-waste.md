Strategy 5 addresses the community's relationship with materials — how goods are purchased, used, reused, and disposed of — through six actions. The plan is candid that this strategy contributes only 0.3% of community-wide emissions reductions in the near term, at a combined cost of approximately $45 million, because calculations are limited to disposal-phase emissions and do not yet capture upstream or embedded emissions from production. The plan explicitly commits to developing a methodology for upstream emissions accounting in future inventory cycles ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]).

The two quantified actions are year-round composting expansion — projected to divert over 5,000 tons of waste annually by 2022, contributing 400 MTCO₂e in modeled GHG reductions at a cost of $26 million — and [[initiatives/commercial-recycling|commercial recycling]] expansion to a 30% diversion rate, projected to reduce 5,600 MTCO₂e (0.3%) at a cost of $18.4 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). Four additional actions with unquantified GHG benefits are included for their co-benefits: [[initiatives/require-sustainable-materials-in-developments|requiring sustainable materials]] in construction and demolition targets 80% diversion of C&D waste from the landfill; [[initiatives/move-toward-circular-economy|moving toward a circular economy]] promotes resource reduction, material reuse, and regeneration through repair centers, tool libraries, thrift stores, and curbside textile collection; [[initiatives/support-plant-rich-diet|supporting plant-rich diets]] through education and potential environmental food labeling at City facilities; and [[initiatives/enhance-refrigerant-recycling-reuse-program|enhancing refrigerant recycling]] targets a 95% reduction in refrigerants disposed of at City facilities ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]).

Year One activity was modest but included two notable initiatives. [[actors/office-of-sustainability-and-innovations|OSI]] launched a [[initiatives/reusable-container-pilot|reusable container pilot]] with four restaurants during Green Fare, testing a model for reducing food container waste in the local restaurant sector ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). OSI also published, in conjunction with the [[actors/department-of-public-works|Public Works Department]], a paper on the circular economy in the journal Sustainability — framing the circular economy as a mechanism to decouple resource consumption from economic activity ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). A cohort was organized for a consumption-based emissions inventory, beginning to build the methodological infrastructure for tracking upstream and embedded emissions beyond disposal-phase accounting ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). Year Two plans include expansion of the reusable container pilot, suggesting the Green Fare model showed sufficient promise to scale ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]).
