Strategy 1 is the single largest contributor to A²ZERO's emissions reduction portfolio, targeting a transition to 100% renewable electricity for the entire community through four complementary actions projected to reduce 41% of community-wide emissions at a combined cost of approximately $4.1 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). The dominant mechanism is [[technology/community-choice-aggregation|Community Choice Aggregation]] (CCA), an opt-out bulk procurement program that would allow the City to purchase 100% new renewable energy on behalf of all residents, businesses, and municipal accounts — projected to reduce 462,100 MTCO₂e (21% of community-wide emissions) without UM participation, rising to 784,000 MTCO₂e (~36%) if the [[actors/university-of-michigan|University of Michigan]] joins. CCA requires state enabling legislation, with the plan's timeline projecting legislation by 2025, a local public vote in 2026, and program launch in 2027. In Year One, the City began working with legislators toward this goal and regularly participated in Michigan Public Service Commission working groups; Year Two plans include continued monitoring, intervention, and testifying before the MPSC and State House and Senate Committees on relevant legislation ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]], [[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]).

The [[initiatives/solarize-ann-arbor|Solarize]] bulk-purchase program for onsite renewable energy targets 78 MW of local generation by 2030 at a program administration cost of $605,000, projected to reduce 85,000 MTCO₂e (3.9%) with UM participation ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). In Year One, Solarize installed approximately 1.3 MW of rooftop solar on over 200 residential roofs, saving residents over $650,000 on upfront costs — a meaningful early demonstration of the bulk-purchase model's viability ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). OSI also launched the [[initiatives/solarize-toolkit|Solarize Toolkit]], a step-by-step guide designed to help other communities replicate the model, and achieved SolSmart Silver designation for efforts to make solar faster, easier, and more affordable. OSI crafted core criteria and principles for achieving the City's renewable energy goals, and helped negotiate agreements on community solar, Voluntary Green Pricing (VGP) programs, and carbon reduction offerings — giving customers more control over their energy sourcing ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). Year Two plans include expansion of Solarize to commercial enterprises ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]).

A [[initiatives/community-solar-program|Community Solar Program]] targeting 11 MW is designed for residents who cannot install rooftop solar — renters, those with shaded roofs, and low-income households — and also requires separate state enabling legislation. Projected: 11,500 MTCO₂e reduced (0.5%) at a cost of $205,000 ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). In Year One, OSI began developing a low-to-moderate income (LMI) solar access program and won a [[actors/national-community-solar-partnership|National Community Solar Partnership]] (NCSP) grant for technical assistance; a [[initiatives/community-solar-pilot|community solar pilot]] and a new [[initiatives/lmi-solar-access-program|LMI solar program]] are both planned for Year Two ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). The [[initiatives/landfill-solar-project|Landfill Solar Project]] — a 24 MW utility-scale installation on the City-owned capped landfill and adjacent [[actors/pittsfield-township|Pittsfield Township]] land, developed in partnership with [[actors/dte-energy|DTE Energy]], incorporating a pilot of [[technology/floating-solar-panels|floating solar panels]] on a retention pond and habitat for local pollinators — was the subject of feasibility and cost studies in Year One. A [[initiatives/municipal-rooftop-solar-rfp|municipal rooftop solar RFP]] was also launched for thirteen City facilities. Projected: 23,000 MTCO₂e reduced (1%) with full operation by 2023 ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]], [[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). The strategy's emissions reduction profile remains heavily back-loaded, with CCA's full impact realized only after its 2027 launch; near-term reductions depend on onsite renewables and landfill solar, making early program execution critical to maintaining momentum.
