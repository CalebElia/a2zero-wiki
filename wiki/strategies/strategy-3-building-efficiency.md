Strategy 3 targets a dramatic improvement in the energy efficiency of all building types — homes, businesses, schools, places of worship, government facilities, and recreational sites — through 11 specific actions projected to reduce community-wide emissions by 13.4% at a combined cost of approximately $14.5 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). The flagship action is a bulk purchase program for energy efficiency retrofits, modeled on the Solarize approach, targeting 85% of owner-occupied homes and 80% of tenant-occupied and commercial buildings achieving a 20% electricity savings and 15% natural gas savings by 2030 — projected to reduce 242,500 MTCO₂e (11.1% of community-wide emissions) with UM participation. The plan notes that as much as 80% of energy consumed in buildings is wasted through envelope gaps and unnecessary operation ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). Supporting programs include the [[initiatives/green-rental-housing-program|Green Rental Housing Program]], which embeds energy efficiency requirements into the existing rental licensing process targeting a 10% reduction in rental property energy use across [[locations/ann-arbor|Ann Arbor]]'s 55% renter-majority housing stock; the [[initiatives/net-zero-energy-affordable-housing|Net Zero Energy Affordable Housing]] initiative, developed in partnership with the [[actors/ann-arbor-housing-commission|Ann Arbor Housing Commission]], aiming to transition all affordable housing sites to net zero energy by 2030; a [[initiatives/loan-loss-reserve|Loan Loss Reserve]] of $1 million to provide credit enhancements for lower-income residents with a 1:20 leverage ratio administered with [[actors/michigan-saves|Michigan Saves]]; LED conversion of all streetlights and traffic signals (including the City gaining ownership of 2/3 of streetlights currently owned by [[actors/dte-energy|DTE Energy]]) targeted for completion by 2029; and the [[initiatives/green-business-challenge|Green Business Challenge]] providing technical assistance and recognition to local businesses targeting 50 participants by 2025 ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]).

Year One activity focused on laying the programmatic and regulatory groundwork for scaled efficiency delivery. OSI completed energy audits of 6 City facilities and participated in scoping for a zero energy fire station design ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). The [[initiatives/aging-in-place-efficiently|Aging in Place Efficiently]] program — integrating energy efficiency upgrades into senior support services, targeting at least 20 low-income seniors reducing energy bills by 15% by 2023 — was designed and piloted in Year One ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]], [[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). A decarbonization initiative was launched with the [[locations/bryant-neighborhood|Bryant neighborhood]] and [[actors/community-action-network|Community Action Network]] to co-create a whole home health, safety, comfort, and decarbonization assessment, embedding equity and community co-design into the efficiency delivery model ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). Year Two plans include the launch of a whole home 'check-up' to help individuals understand their efficiency, electrification, and renewable energy options — likely the Energy Concierge concept from the original plan — as well as energy efficiency improvements at City facilities and solar installations at multiple municipal sites ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]).

On the regulatory side, OSI drafted both a [[initiatives/benchmarking-ordinance|Commercial and Multi-Family Benchmarking Ordinance]] and a home energy disclosure ordinance in Year One, with input from stakeholder engagement ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). These benchmarking and disclosure requirements — targeting 90% compliance across residential, commercial, and institutional sectors by 2025 — are designed as foundational data-gathering tools to drive participation in other efficiency programs ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). OSI also introduced updates to the permitting system to better track energy efficiency progress, worked with the International Code Council (ICC) to introduce Building Code Committees and volunteered to serve on them, and organized a cohort for a consumption-based emissions inventory ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). Building codes remain constrained by state preemption, making the ICC engagement and state-level advocacy critical dependencies for the net zero new construction goal of 2,120 new residential units and 160 new commercial units built to net zero standards by 2030 ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]).
