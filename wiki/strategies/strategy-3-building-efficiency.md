Strategy 3 targets a dramatic improvement in the energy efficiency of all building types — homes, businesses, schools, places of worship, government facilities, and recreational sites — through 11 specific actions projected to reduce community-wide emissions by 13.4% at a combined cost of approximately $14.5 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). The flagship action is a bulk purchase program for energy efficiency retrofits, modeled on the Solarize approach, targeting 85% of owner-occupied homes and 80% of tenant-occupied and commercial buildings achieving a 20% electricity savings and 15% natural gas savings by 2030. The plan notes that as much as 80% of energy consumed in buildings is wasted through envelope gaps and unnecessary operation. Projected: 242,500 MTCO₂e reduced (11.1% of community-wide emissions) with UM participation ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]).

Building codes are identified as a critical lever, but one constrained by state preemption: the plan calls for working with the State of Michigan to adopt the 2021 building code and a zero code appendix requiring all new construction and major renovations to meet net zero energy standards, with a specific goal of 2,120 new residential units and 160 new commercial units built to net zero standards by 2030. Projected: 48,300 MTCO₂e reduced (2.2%) ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). The Green Rental Housing Program addresses Ann Arbor's 55% renter-majority housing stock by embedding energy efficiency requirements into the existing rental licensing process, targeting a 10% reduction in rental property energy use by 2030. The Net Zero Energy Affordable Housing initiative, developed in partnership with the Ann Arbor Housing Commission, aims to transition all affordable housing sites to net zero energy by 2030, with OSI providing direct financial support through 2025. LED conversion of all streetlights and traffic signals — including the City gaining ownership of 2/3 of streetlights currently owned by DTE Energy — is targeted for completion by 2029, contributing 2,600 MTCO₂e at a cost of $3.1 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]).

Several enabling and equity-focused actions round out the strategy. A Loan Loss Reserve of $1 million is proposed to provide credit enhancements for lower-income residents who cannot otherwise finance energy improvements, with a 1:20 leverage ratio administered with Michigan Saves. An Energy Concierge Program would serve as a one-stop navigation service helping residents and businesses identify and access the most impactful and affordable efficiency options, with a goal of assisting 2,500 Ann Arborites by 2025. The Weatherization Expansion Program extends existing County weatherization services to more low-income homeowners, while the Aging in Place Efficiently program — with a vision extending to 2035 — integrates energy efficiency upgrades into senior support services, targeting at least 20 low-income seniors reducing energy bills by 15% by 2023. The Green Business Challenge provides technical assistance and recognition to local businesses, targeting 50 participants by 2025 with 10% reductions in energy consumption, 10% increases in waste diversion, 10% reductions in water consumption, and elimination of single-use plastics. Energy benchmarking and disclosure requirements — targeting 90% compliance across residential, commercial, and institutional sectors by 2025 — are designed as a foundational data-gathering tool to drive participation in other efficiency programs.
