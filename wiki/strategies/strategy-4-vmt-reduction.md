Strategy 4 targets a reduction of at least 50% in vehicle miles traveled (VMT) through seven actions spanning active transportation infrastructure, transit expansion, land use reform, and parking policy. Combined, these actions are projected to reduce just over 8% of community-wide emissions at a cost exceeding $901 million — the most capital-intensive strategy in the plan, driven primarily by transit infrastructure costs ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). The plan notes that transportation emissions are on the rise, making VMT reduction essential to prevent gains in other sectors from being offset.

The two largest investments are transit-focused. Expanding and improving local transit — including bus rapid transit along major corridors, quadrupling current bus ridership, and extending the Get Downtown Program to all [[locations/ann-arbor|Ann Arbor]] employers with more than 20 employees — is projected to reduce 93,200 MTCO₂e (4.3%) at a cost of $656.8 million, with a goal that 95% of residents have transit access within a 10-minute walk at 20-minute frequencies by 2030 ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). Regional transit connecting Ann Arbor to [[locations/detroit|Detroit]], [[locations/ypsilanti|Ypsilanti]], Chelsea, Brighton, and other jurisdictions — serving the 80,000+ daily commuters — is modeled to capture 25% of regional commuting trips by 2030, with work-from-home programs also incorporated into the modeling. Expanding park-and-ride capacity by 10x (from approximately 1,300 to approximately 13,000 spaces) is projected to reduce 28,400 MTCO₂e (1.3%) at a cost of $170 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). The [[initiatives/non-motorized-transportation-plan|Non-Motorized Transportation Plan]] calls for 5 miles of new (primarily protected) bike lanes per year, filling 90% of sidewalk gaps by 2030, and achieving 25% of in-city trips by walking or bicycling — projected to reduce 60,400 MTCO₂e (2.8%) at a cost of $2.4 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). Land use actions address structural drivers of VMT: increasing housing diversity targets 2,000 new multifamily or ADU units by 2030, mixed-use zoning enables daily destinations to be accessible on foot, and tiered parking rates — including dynamic pricing, elimination of parking minimums, and lower rates for EVs and low-income drivers — disincentivize driving while repurposing street space. Transit investments are noted to generate 31% more jobs per dollar than road construction ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]).

Year One activity under this strategy was comparatively limited in scope. [[actors/office-of-sustainability-and-innovations|OSI]] launched a [[initiatives/bike-discount-program|bike discount program]] to reduce barriers to bike access and encourage alternative modes of transportation, coordinated the donation of bikes to [[actors/common-cycle|Common Cycle]], and supported the development of the Vision Zero plan ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). Year Two plans include installation of more bike lanes throughout the City and additional EV and bike charging infrastructure ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). The modest Year One footprint reflects the capital-intensive and multi-agency nature of transit and infrastructure investments, which require longer lead times and external partnerships — particularly with TheRide and regional transit authorities — before significant progress can be reported.
