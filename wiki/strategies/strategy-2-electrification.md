Strategy 2 addresses the conversion of fossil-fuel-powered appliances, heating systems, and vehicles to electric, and is projected to reduce community-wide emissions by 23% at a combined cost of approximately $143 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). The strategy's effectiveness is explicitly contingent on Strategy 1: electrification only achieves its full GHG reduction potential when the electricity powering those systems is sourced from renewables. The dominant action is Home and Business Electrification, which targets 100% of city facilities, 30% of owner-occupied homes, and 25% of rental properties fully electrified by 2030. Natural gas for space heating, water heating, and cooking represents over 25% of community emissions. Projected: 362,200 MTCO₂e reduced (16.5% of community-wide emissions) with UM participation, or 79,200 MTCO₂e (3.6%) without UM ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]).

Vehicle electrification is addressed through four parallel tracks. The EV Bulk Buy Program offers group purchase discounts of 10–15% on new and used EVs to residents and businesses across Washtenaw County, with a goal of 50% of all vehicle miles traveled in EVs by 2030. Projected: 122,900 MTCO₂e reduced (5.6%) at a cost of $700,000 ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). Bus electrification targets TheRide's diesel and diesel-hybrid fleet of 87 buses and UM's bus fleet, purchasing 9 new electric buses per year for TheRide starting in 2021, with a goal of full fleet electrification by 2030. Projected: 13,800 MTCO₂e reduced (0.6%) at a cost of $86 million — the highest per-ton cost in the strategy at $5,839/ton ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). City fleet electrification targets 90% of the municipal fleet by 2025, building on a 2017 City Council resolution directing a Green Fleets Policy update, at a cost of $4 million. Private fleet electrification provides programmatic support and outreach to help medium and large companies transition their fleets to electric, with ridesharing companies targeted for franchise agreements requiring at least half their fleets to be EVs; the goal is 50% of private fleets within the City electric by 2030 ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]).

Expanding EV charging infrastructure is treated as an enabling action rather than a direct emissions reducer. The plan calls for 1,250 new Level 2 chargers per year starting in 2021 and 2 new DC Fast Chargers annually, with a goal that 10% of all public and private parking spaces have Level 2 chargers and 2% have DCFCs. The $42 million cost reflects primarily physical infrastructure. Equity considerations are embedded throughout: EV charger placement prioritizes renters in multifamily housing and low-income areas, used EV bulk buys and e-bike/e-scooter options are explored for affordability, and bus electrification is noted to disproportionately benefit communities of color and low-income riders who suffer higher rates of asthma and COPD along bus corridors.
