Strategy 2 addresses the conversion of fossil-fuel-powered appliances, heating systems, and vehicles to electric, and is projected to reduce community-wide emissions by 23% at a combined cost of approximately $143 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). The strategy's effectiveness is explicitly contingent on Strategy 1: electrification only achieves its full GHG reduction potential when the electricity powering those systems is sourced from renewables. The dominant action is [[initiatives/home-and-business-electrification|Home and Business Electrification]], targeting 100% of city facilities, 30% of owner-occupied homes, and 25% of rental properties fully electrified by 2030. Natural gas for space heating, water heating, and cooking represents over 25% of community emissions. Projected: 362,200 MTCO₂e reduced (16.5% of community-wide emissions) with UM participation, or 79,200 MTCO₂e (3.6%) without UM ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]).

Year One saw meaningful early action on building electrification. [[actors/office-of-sustainability-and-innovations|OSI]] initiated a [[initiatives/heat-pump-replacement-campaign|heat pump replacement campaign]] — including a sticker program to ease the transition process — and developed a [[initiatives/range-replacement-program|range replacement program]] that reduces barriers to [[technology/induction-range|induction range]] adoption through supply and demand side partnerships ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). A collaboration with [[actors/ibew-252|IBEW 252]] was initiated to create a [[initiatives/ibew-252-home-electrification-assessment|home electrification assessment]], embedding organized labor as a partner in the transition. On the vehicle side, 10% of the City's light-duty fleet was transitioned to electric in Year One — including the first EV purchase for the Police Department — against a plan target of 90% of the municipal fleet electrified by 2025, building on a 2017 [[actors/ann-arbor-city-council|City Council]] resolution directing a [[actors/green-fleets-team|Green Fleets]] Policy update, at a cost of $4 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]], [[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). OSI also expanded public EV charging infrastructure and won a grant to install 4 [[technology/dc-fast-charger|Direct Current Fast Chargers (DCFCs)]] at City Hall, while developing and implementing an [[initiatives/ev-readiness-ordinance|EV Readiness Ordinance]] requiring new developments to include EV charging infrastructure in site planning ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]).

Beyond the municipal fleet, vehicle electrification is addressed through additional parallel tracks. The EV Bulk Buy Program offers group purchase discounts of 10–15% on new and used EVs to residents and businesses across [[locations/washtenaw-county|Washtenaw County]], with a goal of 50% of all vehicle miles traveled in EVs by 2030, projected to reduce 122,900 MTCO₂e (5.6%) at a cost of $700,000 ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). [[initiatives/bus-electrification|Bus electrification]] targets [[actors/ann-arbor-area-transit-authority|TheRide]]'s diesel and diesel-hybrid fleet of 87 buses and [[actors/university-of-michigan|UM]]'s bus fleet, purchasing 9 new electric buses per year for TheRide starting in 2021, with a goal of full fleet electrification by 2030 — projected to reduce 13,800 MTCO₂e (0.6%) at a cost of $86 million, the highest per-ton cost in the strategy at $5,839/ton ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). Equity considerations are embedded throughout: EV charger placement prioritizes renters in multifamily housing and low-income areas, and bus electrification disproportionately benefits communities of color and low-income riders who suffer higher rates of asthma and COPD along bus corridors. Year Two plans include the commissioning of the City's first all-electric refuse truck and installation of additional EV and bike charging infrastructure ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]).
