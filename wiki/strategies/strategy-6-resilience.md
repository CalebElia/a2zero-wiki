Strategy 6 focuses on climate adaptation — enhancing the resilience of Ann Arbor's people and place to withstand and recover from climate disruptions — through six actions. While GHG reduction is not the primary purpose of this strategy, the six actions collectively contribute a modest 0.1% reduction in community-wide emissions at a cost of approximately $7.5 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). The plan grounds this strategy in documented local climate impacts: average annual temperature has already risen nearly 1°F since the 1900s (with projections of 3–7°F more by 2050), precipitation has increased 44% since the 1950s, and extreme rainfall events have grown 37% since 1981. Ann Arbor is also projected to have 12–36 more days per year above 90°F by 2050.

The centerpiece action is Resilience Hubs — community-serving facilities augmented with renewable energy, battery storage, and EV charging to support residents before, during, and after natural hazard events. The plan envisions five hubs, at least one per City Ward, co-designed and co-operated with communities, with the first hub targeted for launch in 2022 and subsequent hubs developed through 2023 and beyond. Projected: 2,500 MTCO₂e reduced (0.1%) at a cost of $5.025 million, with the first hubs prioritized in underserved neighborhoods ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). The Neighborhood and Youth Ambassador Program trains residents — including stipended cohorts from affordable housing and low-income communities — to lead local climate action, with a goal of 100 trained ambassadors by 2025.

Tree canopy preservation and expansion targets 10,000 new trees on City property and 10,000 on private property by 2030, contributing 450 MTCO₂e in carbon sequestration while providing urban cooling, biodiversity, and stormwater benefits. The plan notes that tree canopies in low-income communities and communities of color tend to be smaller, and calls for equitable prioritization. Neighborhood asset and needs mapping aims to have every neighborhood in Ann Arbor with an asset map by 2025, identifying safe gathering points and resources for emergencies. Emergency preparedness kit distribution targets 1,000 residents equipped with kits by 2030, with kits integrated into the Ambassador program by 2022. A sensor network for real-time monitoring of heat, air quality, waterways, and flooding — piloted with UM and other partners in 2021 and deployed as an interconnected network by 2024 — is designed to provide proactive risk management data integrated with the City's Smart City infrastructure. Across all six actions, equity is a central design principle: resilience hubs, emergency kits, and ambassador programs are explicitly targeted at frontline and low-income communities who face the greatest climate vulnerability and have the fewest independent resources.
