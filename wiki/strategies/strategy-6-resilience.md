Strategy 6 focuses on climate adaptation — enhancing the resilience of [[locations/ann-arbor|Ann Arbor]]'s people and place to withstand and recover from climate disruptions — through six actions. While GHG reduction is not the primary purpose of this strategy, the six actions collectively contribute a modest 0.1% reduction in community-wide emissions at a cost of approximately $7.5 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). The plan grounds this strategy in documented local climate impacts: average annual temperature has already risen nearly 1°F since the 1900s, precipitation has increased 44% since the 1950s, and extreme rainfall events have grown 37% since 1981, with Ann Arbor projected to have 12–36 more days per year above 90°F by 2050 ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]).

The centerpiece action is [[initiatives/resilience-hubs|Resilience Hubs]] — community-serving facilities augmented with renewable energy, battery storage, and EV charging to support residents before, during, and after natural hazard events. The plan envisions five hubs, at least one per City Ward, co-designed and co-operated with communities, prioritized in underserved neighborhoods, projected to reduce 2,500 MTCO₂e (0.1%) at a cost of $5.025 million ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). In Year One, a resilience hub was created at [[locations/northside-community-center|Northside Community Center]], where a [[technology/battery-storage|solar-powered battery storage]] system provides up to three days of power in case of an outage, and grant funding was secured for the creation of a second hub ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). The [[initiatives/neighborhood-and-youth-ambassador-program|Neighborhood and Youth Ambassador Program]] trains residents — including stipended cohorts from affordable housing and low-income communities — to lead local climate action, with a goal of 100 trained ambassadors by 2025 ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). In Year One, the Ambassadors program was created and 22 community members were trained, who then designed and implemented seven sustainability-focused community projects and developed educational materials for use in their neighborhoods; Year Two plans include training a second cohort ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]).

[[initiatives/tree-canopy|Tree canopy]] preservation and expansion targets 10,000 new trees on City property and 10,000 on private property by 2030, contributing 450 MTCO₂e in carbon sequestration while providing urban cooling, biodiversity, and stormwater benefits. The plan calls for equitable prioritization, noting that tree canopies in low-income communities and communities of color tend to be smaller ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). In Year One, the [[initiatives/10000-trees-initiative|10,000 Trees Initiative]] was developed and launched with over 1,000 trees planted, and grant funding was secured for tree planting specifically in underserved neighborhoods; Year Two plans include the launch of the BranchOut program and more FreeTree Giveaways ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]). Additional resilience actions established in the plan include [[initiatives/asset-and-needs-mapping-of-neighborhoods|neighborhood asset and needs mapping]] (aiming to have every neighborhood with an asset map by 2025), [[initiatives/emergency-preparedness-kits|emergency preparedness kit distribution]] (targeting 1,000 residents equipped with kits by 2030), and [[initiatives/environmental-sensors-monitoring|a sensor network for real-time monitoring of heat, air quality, waterways, and flooding]] — piloted with [[actors/university-of-michigan|UM]] and other partners in 2021 and designed to integrate with the City's Smart City infrastructure ([[sources/cap/cap-2020]]). [[actors/office-of-sustainability-and-innovations|OSI]] also collaborated with the American Society of Adaptation Professionals on a project to analyze climate-based migration into the Great Lakes region, positioning Ann Arbor within a broader regional resilience context ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year1]]).
