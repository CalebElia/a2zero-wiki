The A²ZERO Living Carbon Neutrality Plan is the City of Ann Arbor's foundational strategic document for achieving community-wide carbon neutrality by 2030. It was produced by the City's [[actors/office-of-sustainability-and-innovations|Office of Sustainability and Innovations]] (OSI) under the direction of Mayor [[actors/christopher-taylor|Christopher Taylor]] and City Council, following a unanimous Climate Emergency Declaration on November 4, 2019 (Resolution 19-2103). The plan builds on nearly two decades of local climate action, including the 2012 Climate Action Plan, and was developed over an intensive 82-working-day period — initiated November 13, 2019 with a mid-March 2020 deadline — for public release on Earth Day 2020. It establishes the baseline, the target, and the full portfolio of actions the community must take to eliminate 2.1 million metric tons of CO₂e in annual emissions — the community-wide total recorded in 2018. Importantly, this baseline excludes upstream and embedded emissions from the production of goods and services; the plan commits to developing methodology for capturing those emissions in future inventories. The 2018 emissions breakdown is: [[actors/university-of-michigan|University of Michigan]] (32%), Residential (23%), Commercial/Industrial (22%), Transportation (17%), Solid Waste (3%), and Miscellaneous (3%). The [[actors/university-of-michigan|University of Michigan]]'s participation is modeled throughout the plan's 'win scenario' — which assumes UM mirrors City actions — but was not formally committed at the time of publication, and the plan notes this assumption will change once UM finalizes its own carbon neutrality plan.

The plan is explicitly described as 'living' and iterative, designed to evolve as conditions, technology, and policy change. It rests on three core values — Equity, Sustainability, and Transformation — and frames its approach around two complementary imperatives: avoiding the unmanageable (mitigation) and managing the unavoidable (adaptation), with equity at the center of both. Equity is defined as ensuring everyone gets what they need to succeed based on where they are and where they need to go, with a particular emphasis on not disproportionately burdening low-income, minority, and frontline populations. The plan calls for three companion documents to guide execution: an A²ZERO Investment Plan, an A²ZERO Prioritization Framework, and an A²ZERO Governance Model.

The plan is structured around seven overarching strategies and 44 specific actions. Six strategies address direct emissions sources — renewable grid, electrification, building efficiency, vehicle miles traveled reduction, materials/waste, and resilience — while a seventh 'Other' strategy covers [[initiatives/equity-programs|equity programs]], a community grant program, an internal carbon price, and [[initiatives/carbon-offsets|greenhouse gas offsets]]. Each action is accompanied by a vision statement, responsible parties, collaborators, assumptions, timeline, equity impacts, indicators of success, and quantified cost and GHG reduction estimates where calculable. Ten co-benefits are tracked across actions: supports local economy, produces local energy, improves air quality, creates jobs and/or develops workforce, improves local resilience, improves public health, cost savings accrued, supports biodiversity preservation, benefits the most vulnerable, and scalable or transferable to other communities. Combined, the 44 actions are projected to reduce 2,188,950 MTCO₂e at a total incremental cost of just over $1.056 billion over ten years.

The planning process drew on five pillars: public engagement (68 public events between November 2019 and March 2020, 3 surveys, and outreach including 11,000 school flyers distributed to K–5 students), four Technical Advisory Committees (over 80 volunteers across energy, mobility, resource reduction, and adaptation/resilience), [[actors/a2zero-partner-organizations|67 partner organizations]], staff expertise informed by Project Drawdown and peer city research, and a contracted technical analysis by [[actors/elevate|Elevate Energy]] assessing GHG reduction potential and costs for each action. The plan explicitly acknowledges that a minimum of 15 full-time equivalent staff are needed for implementation, and that staffing decisions are as consequential as funding decisions.
