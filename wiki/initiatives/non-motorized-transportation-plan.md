The [[initiatives/non-motorized-transportation-plan|Implement Non-Motorized Transportation Plan]] initiative aims to ensure an interconnected network of non-motorized options achieving 25% of in-city trips through walking or bicycling by 2030 ([[sources/cap/cap-2020|cap-2020]]). New infrastructure required includes sidewalks, road crossings, separated bike lanes, streetlights, traffic devices, and connections between public transit and active transport modes ([[sources/cap/cap-2020|cap-2020]]). The plan assumes installation of 5 miles of new bike lanes per year (majority protected), at least 5 major mid-block crosswalks and 2 minor mid-block crosswalks per year, and filling 90% of sidewalk gaps by 2030 ([[sources/cap/cap-2020|cap-2020]]). The 10-year cost is estimated at $2,400,000 (staffing, hard, and soft costs), with a GHG reduction potential of approximately 60,000 MTCO²e annually through 2025, declining to zero by 2030 ([[sources/cap/cap-2020|cap-2020]]).

Ann Arbor's non-motorized transportation efforts in Year 3 resulted in the installation of 3.1 miles of new or improved bike lanes and the filling of 2.5 miles of sidewalk gaps, along with installation of two new crosswalks ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year3|a2zero-year3]]). The city was recognized as a Gold-level Walk Friendly community by the Walk Friendly Communities program — one of only 23 cities in the U.S. to receive this designation — and also achieved Gold Bicycle Friendly Community designation from the League of American Bicyclists ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year3|a2zero-year3]]). The [[actors/downtown-development-authority|Ann Arbor Downtown Development Authority]] won the League of Michigan Bicyclists Outstanding Infrastructure Award for most improved bicycle-friendly infrastructure in the past year ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year3|a2zero-year3]]). The City also passed a resolution to restrict turns on red lights in the downtown, reducing vehicle and bicyclist conflicts ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year3|a2zero-year3]]), and initiated a new speed management program to help reduce traffic accidents and advance the City's Vision Zero goal of zero traffic fatalities and serious injuries ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year3|a2zero-year3]]). Looking ahead, the City is supporting expansion of protected bike lanes with 20 or more additional miles slated for development over the coming five years ([[sources/annual-reports/a2zero-year3|a2zero-year3]]).
