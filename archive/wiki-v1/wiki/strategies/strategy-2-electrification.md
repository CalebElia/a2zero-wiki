## Goal

Switch appliances and vehicles from fossil fuels to electric.

## Year 1 (a2zero-year1)

The city converted 10% of its light-duty fleet to electric, including its
first EV purchases for the Police Department. On charging infrastructure,
the city won a grant for 4 Direct Current Fast Chargers at City Hall, and
passed an EV Readiness Ordinance requiring new developments to plan for EV
charging.

Two consumer-facing electrification campaigns launched this year: a heat
pump replacement campaign (with a sticker program to ease the transition)
and a range replacement program reducing adoption barriers for induction
ranges through supply- and demand-side partnerships — see
[[../initiatives/heat-pump-replacement-campaign|Heat Pump Replacement Campaign]]
and [[../initiatives/range-replacement-program|Range Replacement Program]].

A collaboration with electrical union IBEW 252 began this year to develop a
home electrification assessment tool.

## Year 2 (a2zero-year2)

Year 2 showed meaningful scaling in both EV infrastructure and
consumer-facing programs. On EV charging, 40 dual-port chargers were
installed in Downtown Development Authority garages and 4 Direct Current
Fast Chargers went in at City Hall — the DCFC installation fulfills the
Year 1 grant win. The city also received grants for a utility-pole EV
charging pilot ($54,000 from the State of Michigan), reaching 20% fleet
electrification for municipal vehicles (up from 10% in Year 1).

The electric refuse truck commitment was not just met but doubled: the
city won awards for two electric refuse trucks, funded by $270,000 from
the American Lung Association and $406,000 from the State of Michigan —
see [[../commitments/electric-refuse-truck|commitment page]].

Consumer electrification programs expanded significantly: the
Electrification Contractor Training and Badging program launched to vet
and connect residents with qualified professionals — see
[[../initiatives/electrification-contractor-training|Electrification Contractor Training]].
A partnership with IBEW/NECA launched "The Future is Electric" campaign,
and the city joined an RMI cohort of cities working on electrification
campaigns nationally. An Electrification Expo was planned for July 2022.

The Green Rental Housing draft, which includes appliance electrification,
moved through a task force process involving tenants, landlords, and
engineers — see [[../initiatives/green-rental-housing|Green Rental Housing]].
A whole-home decarbonization assessment was developed and piloted in
Bryant, with a Year 3 target of city-wide expansion — see
[[../initiatives/energy-concierge|Energy Concierge]] (linked program).
The permitting system was updated to track electrification transitions
(EVs, heat pumps, stoves) — a quiet but durable infrastructure improvement.

## Year 3 (a2zero-year3)

Year 3's description is explicitly "foundational" — most work set up programs
that will launch or scale in Year 4. The Home Energy Advisor service (formerly
called Energy Concierge) began piloting with Elevate and EcoWorks, providing
needs assessments, contractor referrals, incentive guidance, and customized
decarbonization paths — see [[../initiatives/energy-concierge|Home Energy Advisor]].
Full launch is a Year 4 priority.

The Electrification Expo launched as an annual event (Year 3: 25 vendors, ~450
attendees; second annual planned August 2023). Contractor training advanced
meaningfully: 22 contractors statewide now hold the Michigan Saves
Electrification Badge (created with City assistance). A convening of 30+
contractors across disciplines discussed misconceptions, City programs, and
upcoming rebate program design.

Fleet electrification held at 20% light-duty (same as Year 2). A notable
addition: the City's first heavy electric truck — a fully electric bucket
truck for Signs and Signals — was introduced. The first electric pickups
and vans also joined the light fleet.

The $54K utility-pole EV curbside charging grant (secured in Year 2) moved
toward installation (expected fall 2023). Year 4 still targets 50 more EV
chargers — this has now been a Year 3 and Year 4 priority consecutively
with limited deployment to show. An HOA/MUD toolkit for overcoming solar/EV
installation barriers is under development.

A new heating franchise process launched — a City mechanism to ensure all
residents have access to safe, healthy, clean, and affordable heating —
with a public website, listening sessions, and RFP for ideas. See
[[../initiatives/heating-franchise|Heating Franchise]]. A DOE planning
grant ($580K) was won for a district geothermal loop in Bryant.

## Year 4 (a2zero-year4)

Major milestones this year: the Home Energy Advisor formally launched as a
free city-wide service. The Home Energy Rebate program was designed via a
3-year contract with Elevate, with $4M+ authorized and launch targeted for
summer 2024. The state's first curbside public L2 EV chargers were installed
and operational (from the $54K grant secured Year 3). Fleet electrification
reached 25% (up from 20%), targeting 50% by end of 2024. The state's first
all-electric refuse trucks were formally purchased (the Year 2 "award" became
an actual purchase). Two e-cargo bikes and four e-bikes added to city fleet.

EV charging expanded: 10+ ports at four City parks sites, with one more
upcoming; an EV charger discount program for multi-family/commercial
property owners designed (launching summer 2024); a grant submitted for EV
chargers at two dozen more locations. The AAHC's first EV car share program
launched. City-wide geothermal feasibility study initiated. Bryant district
geothermal design underway — serving 262 households, County Mental Health,
Bryant Elementary, Bryant Community Center, and City public works.

The gas leaf blower phase-out ordinance was supported with a new website and
educational materials; a bulk-buy for electric yard equipment planned late
2024. The heating franchise work continued as a City process. An HOA/MUD
solar and EV toolkit was published. 22 contractors statewide hold Michigan
Saves badges (contractor education continuing).

## Year 5 (a2zero-year5)

**Home Energy Rebates launched** — "over 650 rebates claimed as of May 2025"
covering weatherization, heat pumps, heat pump water heaters, induction
stoves, heat pump dryers, batteries, electrical upgrades, and e-bikes. The
millage-funded program is now live after being designed in Year 4.

The A2ZERO Heat Pump Concierge launched — a new platform for residents to
get design estimates and pricing for furnace-to-heat pump replacements,
including rebate bundling and contractor oversight.

Fleet: city reached 40%+ EV adoption in light duty vehicles; overall city
fleet is now nearly 30% electric. Year 5 target was 70% overall — not met.
The City purchased two more electric refuse trucks (4 total on order); the
first electric refuse truck began providing service in spring 2025.

**Ann Arbor–DTE heating franchise signed on Earth Day, April 22, 2025** —
the first heating franchise in the country to include climate commitments.
An Agreement Regarding Climate Action (ARCA) commits both parties to
cooperate on A2ZERO and DTE climate goals, with a 2-year work plan. This
closes a Years 1–5 open initiative. See [[../initiatives/heating-franchise|Heating Franchise]].

EV charging: $2.8M Charging and Fueling Infrastructure (CFI) grant won from
Federal Highway Administration to expand public EV charging — hiring an EV
coordinator to administer over 4 years. Commercial EV Charger Program
launched: commercial/multifamily owners can receive up to 4 free Level 2
chargers + 5-year software + 5-year warranty. 3rd Annual Home Electrification
Expo held at Farmer's Market.

**Gas leaf blower phase-out began June 1–September 30** (full year-round
prohibition takes effect January 1, 2028). Electric lawn care rebates
launched for both residents and landscaping companies. OSI supported TheRide's
$25M zero-emission bus grant (Federal Transit Administration).

Contractor badging program expanded with Michigan Saves. City sustainability
review actively implemented in plan review process.

## Status as of Year 1

Fleet electrification is still in early single digits relative to the full
city fleet. Consumer programs (heat pump, range replacement) are newly
launched with no outcome data yet — worth watching in Year 2 for adoption
numbers.
