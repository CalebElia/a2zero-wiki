## Goal

A catch-all strategy covering partnership-building, community engagement,
and cross-cutting initiatives that don't map cleanly to Strategies 1–6.

## Year 1 (a2zero-year1)

The city secured 92 partner organizations as collaborators in Year 1 — the
single largest network-building number in the report. It launched RE4M, an
internal 4-part learning series on integrating renewable energy,
efficiency, electrification, and equity, and supported the creation of the
city's equitable engagement initiative.

The Sustaining Ann Arbor Together grant program continued, funding
community-driven sustainability projects. A2ZERO was named a finalist for
the America's Climate Leadership Award, and the city launched an A2ZERO
Pledge for residents to formally commit to the plan's goals.

Public engagement in Year 1 was extensive: a Sustainability Forum series,
Climate Coffee and Conversation discussions, a revamped website, a new
YouTube channel, A2ZERO Week (marking the plan's one-year anniversary),
neighborhood association meetings, a Farmer's Market presence, the 51st
annual Earth Day celebration, an inaugural Juneteenth celebration with the
Ann Arbor NAACP branch, and conference presentations at the 2021 Michigan
Sustainability Conference and the Washtenaw Community College Climate
Summit.

Institutionally, the city participated in the Environmental Rules Review
Committee, joined committees developing the state's MI Healthy Climate
Plan, served on U-M's Presidents Commission on Carbon Neutrality, and
launched an internal Carbon Neutrality Coordinating Committee.

## Year 2 (a2zero-year2)

The collaborator network grew from 92 (Year 1) to over 110 organizations —
steady growth suggesting the network is retaining members and adding new
ones rather than churning. The city hosted over 100 community events,
meetings, and presentations this year.

Recognition accelerated: the city was named to CDP's "A List" of 95
communities worldwide (for emissions transparency, targets, and risk
planning), and earned a Gold Level from Michigan Green Communities. Multiple
staff members received individual awards: Missy Stults named by GRIST as
one of 50 "Fixers" of 2022; Julie Roth awarded the George Sexton Public
Servant of the Earth Award; Sean Reynolds awarded the Governmental Award
for Urban and Community Forestry; Simi Barr recognized by Crain's as an EV
champion; Galen Hardy appointed to the Great Lakes Renewable Energy
Association board. The proposed Sustainable Energy Utility won project of
the year from GLREA.

Grant activity was heavy: the city raised ~$8.7 million across Year 2
including $4.5M from ARPA for city facility solar, $2.5M for affordable
housing net-zero, $270K+$406K for electric refuse trucks, $170K for DCFC
chargers, $75K from McKnight Foundation for Bryant, $25K from EPA for
Bryant, $54K for utility-pole EV charging, and smaller grants for trees,
contractor training, and electrification expo.

The Bryant neighborhood decarbonization initiative was described in Year 2
as aiming to create "the first fully decarbonized existing neighborhood in
America" — the most ambitious framing yet — see
[[../topics/bryant-neighborhood-decarbonization|Bryant Neighborhood Decarbonization]].

## Year 3 (a2zero-year3)

The headline event: in November, Ann Arborites passed a 20-year Community
Climate Action Millage — making Ann Arbor the 8th community in the US to
tax itself to fund local climate action. This creates a multi-decade funding
stream for A2ZERO programs and is the most structurally significant
governance milestone in the plan's history — see
[[../initiatives/community-climate-action-millage|Community Climate Action Millage]].
Year 4 rebate and incentive programs are planned to be funded by it.

For the second consecutive year, Ann Arbor made CDP's "A List" (now as one
of 122 cities globally, up from 95). Gold-level Michigan Green Communities
recognition continued. VP Kamala Harris visited to discuss sustainability
activities. Three OSI staff graduated from Champions for Change (racial
equity training); five total have completed it.

The A2ZERO Collaborators network grew to 120+ organizations (from 110 in
Year 2), fulfilling the Year 2 commitment. New engagement model: virtual
and in-person events designed to activate collaborators as planning and
implementation partners rather than passive members.

Community events: 150+ events, meetings, and presentations (up from 100+
in Year 2). The Solarize Toolkit was updated and published; OSI was
recognized with the GLREA Exemplary Partnership Award. A schools grant
program launched for public school sustainability projects. A regional
climate collaborative feasibility study began with Washtenaw County and
regional stakeholders — partial progress toward the county-wide climate
action commitment.

Grants secured in Year 3: $580K DOE for Bryant geothermal, $500K MI-HOPE
for Bryant decarbonization, $229K AmeriCorps (10 members for engagement),
$980K SEMCOG for LED streetlights, $200K SEMCOG for Washington St Bike
Boulevard, $180K EECBG for Bryant Community Center carbon negative, $54K
for utility-pole EV charging, $20K for equitable circular economy engagement,
and more.

## Year 4 (a2zero-year4)

The A2ZERO Dashboard launched — tracking plan progress publicly. This
resolves the metrics dashboard commitment from Year 2 that had been marked
"missed" after Year 3; it arrived two years late but fully. See
[[../initiatives/a2zero-dashboard|A2ZERO Dashboard]] and
[[../commitments/metrics-dashboard-launch|commitment update]].

The Ann Arbor Climate Corps launched as a national AmeriCorps program
(education and behavior change focus). OSI's communications infrastructure
expanded significantly: new Instagram and Facebook channels; Green Light
recurring TV show on CTN; recurring segment on 107.1 radio; Strategic
Communication Plan and Branding Guidelines developed.

The Sustaining Ann Arbor Together grant program was formalized with $80K
granted. The A2ZERO Community Collaborators network reached 130+ organizations
(from 120 in Year 3). Hosted 100+ community meetings, events, and presentations.
CDP A List recognition continued (119 cities globally; Ann Arbor included).
Michigan Green Communities Gold Level continued.

Staff recognition: Julie Roth named Crain's Detroit 2023 Notable Leader in
Energy; Missy Stults named Constellation's 50 Notable Sustainability Leaders
of 2024. Staff served on 30+ national conference presentations.

Regional: Continued regional climate collaborative feasibility work with
Washtenaw County and SEMCOG. Staff joined SEMCOG Green Task Force and EV
Infrastructure Roundtable steering committee.

## Year 5 (a2zero-year5)

Ann Arbor Climate Corps: now 14 AmeriCorps members. 8 completed full terms
(13,600 hours). Farmer's Market tabling (418 residents engaged). Door-
knocking campaign: 1,000+ households reached with rebate and incentive info.
AmeriCorps supported stewardship on 53.7 acres of Natural Areas.

**200+ discrete community events** in Year 5. A2ZERO Week: 510 attendees;
400 at OSI tent at SummerFest; 30 events; 15+ co-hosts; 540 Zero Waste
pledges. Cinema and Sustainability series: 4 films, 250+ attendees.
Sustainability Forums at AADL: 100+ in-person, 50+ virtual. First Net Zero
Home Tour during A2ZERO Week.

**Recognition:** OSI won "Best of the Midwest" Climate Action Museum award.
Ann Arbor received an **A score from CDP** (formerly Carbon Disclosure
Project). Ann Arbor achieved **Platinum status** in Michigan Green Communities
program (only 2 cities in state at this level). 50+ conference presentations.
Near-100 media interviews including Time Magazine and BBC.

A2ZERO newsletter: 4,502 subscribers; 46% open rate. Instagram + Facebook
grew 750+ followers. Green Light TV: 9 episodes, 2,000 combined views. Weekly
107.1 radio segment. OSI staff appointed to Washtenaw 2030 District Board.

Sister City delegation from Tubingen, Germany (exclusive focus on carbon
neutrality pathways). Collaboration formalized with Pittsfield Village
community for decarbonization. Youth Now Climate Action created.

Capital Improvements Planning Process update underway to integrate
sustainability criteria. National conversations on climate-based migration.

The City's MPSC interventions over 4 years have saved ratepayers more than
**$1 billion** — a figure that dramatically contextualizes the value of
City utility advocacy beyond its direct emissions impact.

## Status as of Year 1

This strategy functions as connective tissue for the other six — its
metrics (92 partners, multiple new committees) are about building capacity
and legitimacy rather than direct emissions reduction. Useful to track
whether the partner count keeps growing in later years as a proxy for
program momentum.
