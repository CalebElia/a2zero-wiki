## Goal

Significantly improve energy efficiency in buildings, both municipal and
residential.

## Year 1 (a2zero-year1)

The city completed energy audits on 6 City facilities and began drafting
two ordinances: a commercial/multi-family benchmarking ordinance and a home
energy disclosure ordinance, both shaped by stakeholder engagement.

Two notable equity-focused programs launched this year. First, the Aging in
Place Efficiently pilot, aimed at improving efficiency and comfort in older
adults' homes — see
[[../initiatives/aging-in-place-efficiently|Aging in Place Efficiently]].
Second, and most relevant to ongoing Bryant neighborhood research: a
decarbonization initiative launched with the Bryant neighborhood and
Community Action Network to co-create a whole-home health, safety, comfort,
and decarbonization assessment — see
[[../topics/bryant-neighborhood-decarbonization|Bryant Neighborhood Decarbonization]].

Other Year 1 work: updated permitting system tracking for energy
efficiency progress, collaboration with the International Code Council on
Building Code Committees, scoping for a zero-energy fire station design,
and organizing a cohort for a consumption-based emissions inventory.

## Year 2 (a2zero-year2)

Year 2's headline achievement was passing Michigan's first commercial
benchmarking ordinance, requiring large commercial and multifamily buildings
to track and report energy and water performance annually, with the first
deadline set for June 1 — see
[[../initiatives/commercial-benchmarking-ordinance|Commercial Benchmarking Ordinance]].
The ordinance is expected to generate average energy savings of 2–3% per year.

The Aging in Place Efficiently program moved from co-design to service
delivery, serving 19 older, income-qualified residents through combined
energy efficiency, mobility, and home safety improvements — see
[[../initiatives/aging-in-place-efficiently|Aging in Place Efficiently]].
A Home Energy Rating Disclosure program reached draft stage and is expected
to go to Council in Year 3 — see
[[../initiatives/home-energy-rating-disclosure|Home Energy Rating Disclosure]].

The Bryant whole-home decarbonization assessment was developed and is being
piloted in Year 2, with a city-wide expansion target for Year 3 — see
[[../topics/bryant-neighborhood-decarbonization|Bryant Neighborhood Decarbonization]].
The city also conducted energy audits on 4 City facilities and began
developing decarbonization plans for them. (Note: Year 1 wiki records 6
audits completed in Year 1; Year 2 reports 4 more — see
[[../contradictions/city-facility-audit-count|contradiction flag]] for
discussion of how these figures relate.)

Other notable Year 2 work: $2.5 million secured with Ann Arbor Housing
Commission to transition affordable housing sites to net zero; collaboration
with the International Code Council continued toward adopting the 2021
model building code; a zero-energy fire station design was advanced pending
funding; and a Sustainability Review was integrated into the city's plan
review process.

**Prior-year commitment check:**

- Municipal facility efficiency → partial (4 facilities audited, decarbonization plans in progress)

## Year 3 (a2zero-year3)

Year 3 saw the Commercial Benchmarking Ordinance fully implemented — the first
in Michigan — see [[../initiatives/commercial-benchmarking-ordinance|Commercial
Benchmarking Ordinance]]. The Home Energy Rating Disclosure policy was fully
drafted for home sales at point of listing, with Council introduction
targeted for summer 2023 — see [[../initiatives/home-energy-rating-disclosure|HERD]].

Aging in Place Efficiently served 17 more participants (Year 2: 19) with
energy efficiency and home safety retrofits, saving $45,800 in utility costs
and reducing 113 metric tons of carbon emissions — then **closed** as its
pilot phase ended. Ongoing grant funding is being sought — see
[[../initiatives/aging-in-place-efficiently|Aging in Place Efficiently]].

The Bryant neighborhood received $500K (MI-HOPE) and $180K (EECBG) specifically
for decarbonization work — 19 households receiving health, safety, and quality
of life improvements — see
[[../topics/bryant-neighborhood-decarbonization|Bryant Neighborhood Decarbonization]].
The $180K will make Bryant Community Center a carbon-negative building and
living demonstration site.

Decarbonization audits began at four municipal buildings (goal: full
decarbonization by 2025), clarifying the Year 1/2 audit count ambiguity —
see [[../contradictions/city-facility-audit-count|contradiction page]] for update.
A $300K grant was provided to local housing providers for energy efficiency
improvements for lower-income residents. The city also secured $3M in federal
aid (with AAHC) for net-zero affordable housing and won a streetlight LED
conversion grant ($980K from SEMCOG, starting fall 2023) — see
[[../initiatives/led-streetlight-conversion|LED Streetlight Conversion]].

A Green Business Challenge is being designed with SPARK, and workforce
development conversations with IBEW 252 were initiated.

## Year 4 (a2zero-year4)

Two long-running commitments finally resolved this year. The Home Energy
Rating Disclosure (HERD) launched with two hired assessors, and the City
became a DOE Home Energy Score Partner — see
[[../initiatives/home-energy-rating-disclosure|HERD]]. The Green Rental
Housing program made "significant progress" with a Council presentation
planned for summer/fall 2024 — still not enacted, but closer than any
prior year.

The Home Energy Advisor launched city-wide (also counted under Strategy 2).
$4M+ authorized for residential rebates (3-year Elevate contract, launching
summer 2024). Weatherization services expanded via a grant to Washtenaw
County OCED for income-qualified residents. LED retrofit completed at
Wheeler Service Center.

Decarbonization assessments were conducted at six municipal facilities
with carbon neutrality plans developed — up from four in Year 3. This
confirms Year 4 added new facilities beyond the four Year 3 buildings
(or changed the scope). See the updated
[[../contradictions/city-facility-audit-count|contradiction page]].

The Green Business Challenge launched in partnership with SPARK. A 3-year
benchmarking contract with Washtenaw 2030 District launched. A Bryant
neighborhood 3-year contract with Community Action Network started.
$1.3M secured for Bryant decarbonization. A city-wide networked geothermal
study initiated.

## Year 5 (a2zero-year5)

Home Energy Advisor: helped 300+ residents get free home energy assessments
with customized decarbonization plans. Home Energy Rebates: hundreds of
thousands of dollars distributed (650+ claims across all rebate types).

**Green Rental Housing brought to City Council** — "After years of
engagement, the City brought forward the Green Rental Housing ordinance
for consideration by Council." Not yet enacted; Year 6 still lists it.
This is the fifth year the program has appeared as a priority.

Michigan updated its building codes (early 2025) — does not apply to the
Residential Code, but work underway for a residential update. Michigan
legislature also banned HOAs from restricting on-site solar, heat pumps,
clotheslines, and other energy efficiency measures — a significant statewide
policy win for A2ZERO's model.

**Home Energy Rating Disclosure: 700 homes received completed Home Energy
Scores** this year — the program is now in full stride after launching in
Year 4.

Commercial Benchmarking Ordinance: major push to bring all buildings >20K
sq ft into compliance, in partnership with Washtenaw 2030 District.

Green Business Challenge: growing; hired 4 interns; Fresh Coast Climate
Solutions brought in for individualized business work plans. Still working
toward 50 businesses enrolled.

Bryant neighborhood: $3.1M MPSC grant won for energy efficiency and
electrification improvements. Foundational work underway for Year 6 delivery.
RFP released for contractors (RFP 25-21). McKnight Foundation continuing
support via Community Action Network.

LED streetlights: "Work started in earnest" on city-wide conversion thanks
to Year 3 SEMCOG grant + additional funding. Wheeler Service Center complete;
full city conversion advancing. Avalon Housing (Stimson Apartments, 23 units)
received technical and financial support for energy efficiency improvements.
AAHC affordable housing at 121 Catherine Street being supported.

County OCED weatherization: OSI provided funding to expand services for
income-qualified households, enabling higher investment per household in
decarbonization approaches (heat pumps, appliances, more).
