## Goal

Power Ann Arbor's electrical grid with 100% renewable energy, as part of the
A2ZERO carbon neutrality plan adopted by City Council in June 2020.

## Year 1 (a2zero-year1)

The city's first year of work centered on residential solar deployment and
groundwork for larger structural programs. About 1.3MW of rooftop solar went
in across 200+ residential roofs, saving residents a combined $650,000 in
upfront costs. The city packaged its approach as the "Solarize Toolkit" so
other communities could replicate the model — see
[[../initiatives/solarize-toolkit|Solarize Toolkit]].

On the policy side, the city negotiated agreements covering community solar,
Voluntary Green Pricing, and carbon reduction offerings, aimed at giving
residents more choice over their energy sourcing. A
low-to-moderate-income (LMI) solar access program began development this
year, backed by a National Community Solar Partnership grant for technical
assistance — see [[../initiatives/lmi-solar-access-program|LMI Solar Access Program]].

The city also studied the feasibility of a large 24MW solar installation on
a capped landfill, and issued an RFP for rooftop solar across thirteen
municipal facilities. SolSmart Silver designation was achieved, and the city
stayed active in Michigan Public Service Commission working groups.

## Year 2 (a2zero-year2)

Year 2 was a step-change in both deployment scale and structural leverage.
On the residential side, the Solarize program added 1.7MW — bringing the
two-year cumulative to over 3MW across 430+ households, with residents
saving a collective $1.5 million in upfront costs and a projected $15
million over system lifetimes. The city also developed a Solarize Toolkit
for the Great Lakes Renewable Energy Association, codifying the model for
export beyond Washtenaw County — see
[[../initiatives/solarize-toolkit|Solarize Toolkit]]. A pilot commercial
Solarize program was designed (not yet launched) in collaboration with the
2030 District.

The most consequential Year 2 action was not solar deployment but utility
policy: the city intervened in and settled utility case U-20713, securing
three concurrent gains — (1) the first community solar offering in DTE's
territory, anchored at Wheeler Center Solar Park; (2) a working group on
24/7 renewable power and Virtual Power Reduction Agreements; and (3) reduced
MIGreenPower rates for enrollees. A second utility case, U-20836, challenged
DTE's proposed rate increase and solar compensation changes. These
interventions fulfill the Year 1 commitments for both a community solar
pilot and a utility carbon-offering working group.

The Wheeler Center Solar Park — a 20MW solar field at the former Ann Arbor
landfill — reached final design this year; see
[[../initiatives/wheeler-center-solar-park|Wheeler Center Solar Park]].
The city also designed 4MW of rooftop solar for municipal facilities
(water treatment, water recovery, parks, and others), with installations
scheduled to begin summer 2022. A Sustainable Energy Utility concept was
advanced — a proposed community-owned entity delivering 100% local solar
and battery storage — see
[[../initiatives/sustainable-energy-utility|Sustainable Energy Utility]].

## Year 3 (a2zero-year3)

Year 3 was the most active solar deployment year yet. The Solarize program
installed over 4MW of rooftop solar, representing 540 new residential
installations — more than all prior years combined by volume. The commercial
Solarize program launched (in partnership with the 2030 District), fulfilling
the long-pending commitment from Year 1. Year 4 continues to target 5MW,
suggesting the 5MW milestone is either just ahead or the target has shifted
to an annual rate rather than a cumulative one — see the note in the
[[../initiatives/solarize-toolkit|Solarize Toolkit]] page.

On City facilities, 4MW of solar is now under contract: nearly 1MW at parks
facilities (installations underway), and ~3MW at Wheeler Service Center,
Water/Wastewater Treatment Plants, and the municipal airport (construction
starts fall 2023). A separate City/UM joint RFP targets 1MW additional on
City sites and up to 20MW at UM sites. A $2.5 million federal grant was
secured for solar and battery storage on critical City facilities.

The Wheeler Center Solar Park (20MW landfill installation) remained in
progress — no groundbreaking in Year 3, carried forward to Year 4. The city
also submitted a DOE grant to formally create the Sustainable Energy Utility
(decision expected mid-2024) and commissioned an Energy Options Analysis
comparing SEU viability, traditional municipalization, and other pathways —
see [[../initiatives/sustainable-energy-utility|Sustainable Energy Utility]].

Utility intervention continued: submitted testimony in U-20836 (rate case)
and intervened in three additional MPSC cases (U-21172, U-21297, U-21376)
to improve renewable opportunities, decrease rates, and improve reliability.
The city also supported community solar legislation and a bill to remove the
1% distributed generation cap in the Michigan legislature.

Two geothermal studies launched: district geothermal design around Veterans
Park (NREL clean energy technical assistance) and a DOE planning grant for
a district geothermal loop in Bryant ($580K) — see
[[../initiatives/district-geothermal|District Geothermal]].

## Year 4 (a2zero-year4)

Solar deployment split into two tracks this year. On City facilities, 250kW
was installed across seven parks/recreation sites (Leslie Park Golf Course,
Huron Hills Golf Course, Cobblestone Farm, Buhr Park, Ann Arbor Senior
Center, Gallup Canoe Livery, Fuller Park). Separately, nearly 4MW of
additional City facility solar is in design for eight more sites (Municipal
Airport, Municipal Center, Wheeler Service Center, Water Resource Recovery
Facility, Veterans Memorial Park, Fire Stations 3 and 6, Bicentennial Park),
targeted for Year 5 installation. A 500kW battery at the Water Recovery
Facility is also underway. The $2.5M federal grant for solar and storage at
critical City sites was deployed. A $70K microgrid design grant and a
separate federal submission target Veterans Memorial Park and Fire Station 3.

On Solarize, 231 systems (1.8MW) were installed in calendar year 2023 —
the highest single-year installation count. Cumulative total: over 4.8MW
across 630+ households. Year 5 target: 5.5MW. See
[[../initiatives/solarize-toolkit|Solarize Toolkit]].

Wheeler Center Solar Park: no groundbreaking in Year 4. Strategy is being
reconsidered ("continuing to strategize and adjusting plan"). A $5M award
was secured to advance solar at/around the landfill site. This is the
fourth year without breaking ground — see
[[../initiatives/wheeler-center-solar-park|Wheeler Center Solar Park]].

The renewable energy pathways work culminated in: (1) the 100% Renewable
Energy Pathways study published; (2) Council authorized a Phase II
Municipalization Study in May 2024; (3) the SEU going to voters in
November 2024. See [[../initiatives/sustainable-energy-utility|SEU]].
A city-wide networked geothermal study also launched. The state signed
clean energy legislation (SB 271) requiring 50% clean electricity by 2030
and 100% by 2040 — major policy tailwind for all of A2ZERO's goals.

## Year 5 (a2zero-year5)

The headline achievement: **Ann Arbor voters approved creating the SEU with
79% of the vote on November 5, 2024** — a landmark governance milestone.
Work immediately began; the SEU is expected to begin offering service 24
months after the vote. $5M was secured to fund foundational tools,
structures, and preliminary staffing. A waitlist was launched with biweekly
updates. Year 6 priority: "Stand up the SEU."

On city facility solar: 700kW of new solar installed this year across
Veterans Memorial Park, Fuller Park, and the Ann Arbor Airport — bringing
total city facility generating capacity above 1MW. Additional solar designed
for Year 6 (Bicentennial Park, Fire Station 1, City Hall, Election Center,
Wheeler Service Center, Steere Farm, Water Resource Recovery Facility, more).
Battery storage systems at the Water Recovery Facility and Fire Station 6
planned for Year 6. For the first time: 70% of electricity powering City
facilities and operations now comes from renewables, via a Constellation
Energy contract under Michigan's Electric Customer Choice Program (67 more
city sites enrolled this year).

On Solarize: reached 5.4MW — just short of the 5.5MW Year 5 target but a
meaningful milestone. Solarize expanded statewide including Grand Rapids.

The Bryant geothermal system was designed for 262 households + Bryant
Community Center + Bryant Elementary + County Mental Health + City public
works. **OSI won a $10M DOE grant** to begin installation — but as of report
publication, funding is on hold due to federal policy action. The City is
pursuing resolution. Geothermal was also installed at the Bryant Community
Center itself. Geothermal was integrated into new affordable housing at
Catherine Street (in collaboration with AAHC). City-wide geothermal
feasibility study with Stantec initiated (18-month timeline).

Net-zero fire station: **groundbreaking occurred May 20** — the City's first
net-zero energy fire station.

OSI intervened in two gas cases before the MPSC for the first time, calling
for a "Future of Gas" proceeding to outline a path to decarbonized heating
in DTE's service territory. Over 4 years of MPSC interventions have saved
ratepayers more than **$1 billion** in aggregate.

SB 271 (Michigan, signed 2023): 50% clean by 2030, 100% by 2040 — major
structural tailwind. Drafted community choice aggregation legislation and
supported microgrid legislation for state legislature.

## Status as of Year 1

Early-stage: most work this year was residential pilots, RFPs, and program
design rather than large-scale deployment. The landfill solar project and
the municipal RFP are the two highest-potential-impact items to watch in
Year 2 (a2zero-year1, "Next Steps").
