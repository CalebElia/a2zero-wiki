## Goal

Reduce vehicle miles traveled by at least 50%.

## Year 1 (a2zero-year1)

This was the thinnest strategy in Year 1 relative to the others — three
items reported: a bike discount program to lower the cost barrier to
cycling, a donation of bikes to Common Cycle, and support for development
of the city's Vision Zero plan (a road-safety framework, not exclusively a
climate program, but linked here as it affects mode-shift incentives).

## Year 2 (a2zero-year2)

Still the thinnest strategy by reported activity. Year 2 added five items:
a collaboration with the Planning Department to integrate A2ZERO into the
Unified Development Code; support for expansion of protected bike lanes
(fulfilling the Year 1 commitment); continuation of the bike discount
program with local vendors; initiation of a "carbon calculator" using
transportation metrics in the plan review process; and the Planning
Department's removal of parking minimums from plan review requirements.

The parking-minimum removal is arguably the most structurally significant
item — reducing parking requirements tends to shift modal mix over time —
but it is reported as a Planning Department action rather than an A2ZERO
program, so its direct attribution to this strategy may be partial.

The UDC integration work and carbon calculator are early-stage in Year 2
and will be worth tracking for concrete policy changes in Year 3.

**Prior-year commitment check:**
- Bike lane installation → fulfilled ("Supported expansion of protected bike lanes")

## Year 3 (a2zero-year3)

Year 3 was by far the strongest for this strategy, which had been the
consistent laggard. Concrete infrastructure delivered: 3.1 miles of new
or improved bike lanes, 2.5 miles of sidewalk gaps filled, two new
crosswalks, and a downtown red-light turn restriction reducing vehicle/
cyclist conflicts. Larger structural commitments: the community passed a
transit millage to support TheRide (takes effect 2024), the City rezoned
~400 acres to TC1 (transit corridor) status, and an AAATA propulsion
study launched for carbon-free busing.

A Comprehensive Plan RFP was released with carbon neutrality and equity
as explicit priorities — the most direct integration of A2ZERO into land
use planning yet, though the plan update itself is a multi-year process.
A new speed management program for Vision Zero also advanced.

Recognition: Ann Arbor received Gold-level Walk Friendly and Bicycle
Friendly Community designations; the DDA won the League of Michigan
Bicyclists Outstanding Infrastructure Award.

Key context: transportation remains 30% of local GHG emissions
(a2zero-year3, GHG Summary), and vehicle miles returned to only 3% below
2018 levels by 2022 after the COVID-era drop — meaning meaningful VMT
reduction has not yet materialized. Infrastructure investment is accelerating
but its emissions impact won't show for years.

## Year 4 (a2zero-year4)

Year 4 continued the strong VMT strategy momentum from Year 3. Infrastructure
highlights: 12.1 miles of bike facilities and 21 bike intersection
improvements funded or supported; 1.39 miles of sidewalk gaps filled;
13 new crosswalks; bicycle parking pilot (4 installations). The shared
micromobility program returned with 450 scooters and 100 e-bikes — logging
214,000+ trips in 2023 (21% year-over-year increase). Downtown bikeways
attracted 100,000+ users in just five months (September–January).

Key policy advances: parking minimums eliminated from most developments
(city-wide, not just downtown); new transit corridor zoning districts
created; comprehensive land use plan update underway with affordability,
equity, and sustainability as core tenants; dashboards launched for
sidewalk gaps, traffic calming, and Vision Zero progress.

TheRide: zero-emissions propulsion study supported; grant submitted for
first zero-emission buses. Transit millage effects begin in 2024.

Ann Arbor retained both Gold Walk Friendly and Gold Bicycle Friendly
Community designations. City Hall received Silver-level Bicycle Business
certification.

- Land use and UDC A2ZERO integration → partial (Comp Plan update underway; parking minimums eliminated; new zoning districts; full UDC integration ongoing)

## Year 5 (a2zero-year5)

OSI's primary Year 5 focus: supporting the city's draft Comprehensive Land
Use Plan, one of whose three core tenants is sustainability. Public comment
open at time of publication.

Bike infrastructure: continued collaboration with transportation team on
crosswalks, lighting, bike lane expansion, and bike parking expansion.
New program: "Borrow a Cargo Bike A2" launched in collaboration with Human
Electric Hybrids and Bicycle Alliance of Washtenaw. OSI itself purchased a
cargo bike for event transport.

Ann Arbor retained Gold-level Walk Friendly (one of only 20 nationwide) and
Gold Bicycle Friendly Community designations for the second consecutive year.

TheRide: successfully supported the $25M zero-emission bus grant (FTA No
Emission Bus program) — a major win for the transit electrification side of
this strategy.

Dashboards continue to track sidewalk gaps, traffic calming, and Vision Zero.

Year 5 had lighter Year 4 infrastructure numbers — focus was on policy
(Comp Plan) and existing programs rather than new construction.

## Status as of Year 1

Notably light progress compared to the other 6 strategies — no major
infrastructure, ordinance, or program launches reported, only a small pilot
and supporting roles on adjacent city plans. Worth tracking in Year 2
whether this strategy accelerates or remains the laggard.
